# Organic Review Style

Use these rules for organic chemistry, organometallic chemistry, catalysis, synthetic methodology, allene chemistry, propargylic chemistry, stereochemical control, and mechanism-focused papers.

## Voice And Diction

- Write formal academic review prose, not an abstract, sales pitch, or experiment log.
- Lead with the chemical conclusion or transformation identity.
- Prefer precise verbs: `reported`, `observed`, `showed`, `demonstrated`, `supported`, `proposed`, `interpreted`, `furnished`, `delivered`, `afforded`, or `elucidated`.
- Use `demonstrated` only when the paper provides direct evidence or a clear experimental result.
- Use `is consistent with`, `is proposed to proceed via`, `may involve`, or `was interpreted as` for indirect mechanism or author inference.
- Avoid ornamental hedging such as "it is interesting to note" and generic openings such as "recent years have seen".
- Do not write from the source authors' perspective. Replace "we developed" with "the authors developed" or a citation-neutral construction.

## Organic Chemistry Information To Preserve

For a single-paper paragraph, preserve these details when source-supported and review-relevant:

- Reaction class, activation mode, catalyst family, substrate class, coupling partner or nucleophile, and product substitution pattern.
- Bond-forming or bond-breaking event, named reaction family, starting-material class, product class, and synthetic purpose when they define the method identity.
- Catalyst, ligand, additive, promoter, amine, oxidant, reductant, photocatalyst, electrolyte, or Lewis/Bronsted acid role when it changes the chemistry.
- Full catalytic system when ligand, counterion, additive, terminal oxidant/reductant, photosensitizer, mediator, solvent, or stoichiometric activator controls reactivity or selectivity.
- Preparative scale, catalyst loading, reagent equivalents, solvent, temperature, and atmosphere when the source frames the protocol as gram-scale, 10 mmol scale, facile, practical, or operationally simple.
- Yield range, representative yield, ee/er, dr, rr, regioisomeric ratio, conversion, TON/TOF, scale, catalyst loading, or practicality metric when central to the claim.
- Substrate class contrasts: aryl vs alkyl, terminal vs internal, aldehyde vs ketone, protected vs unprotected, electron-rich vs electron-poor, hindered vs unhindered, heteroaryl tolerance, or functional-group tolerance.
- Main limitation: narrow scope, stoichiometric activator, harsh condition, high loading, air/moisture sensitivity, weak selectivity, low conversion, scale limitation, missing controls, or unresolved mechanism.

## Mechanistic Precision

- Distinguish reaction class, activation mode, catalyst family, substrate class, nucleophile/coupling partner, and product topology.
- Name elementary steps only when supported: oxidative addition, ionization, Lewis acid activation, pi-acid activation, nucleophilic attack, transmetalation, migratory insertion, reductive elimination, protonolysis, radical addition, or SET.
- Do not promote a plausible catalytic cycle into fact. Mechanistic prose must inherit the evidence strength assigned during extraction.
- Pair each mechanistic noun with an evidence-status modifier unless direct observation, decisive trapping, kinetics, labeling, spectroscopy, or computation supports stronger wording.
- Do not move conditions, catalyst loading, solvent, temperature, time, or selectivity values from one paper to another.
- If the paper lacks mechanistic experiments, write that the mechanism was proposed or inferred rather than established.

## Scope Compression

- Do not reproduce a substrate table in prose.
- Compress scope into class-level trends, boundaries, and one representative anchor if needed.
- A useful scope sentence names substrate class, derivative class, coupling partner or nucleophile class, product class, yield/selectivity range, and a limitation when those are available.
- Do not call a method broad, general, efficient, mild, or highly selective without numbers or demonstrated class-level support.
- Keep negative examples or weak substrate classes when they define the method boundary.
- Count examples only after classifying what they show: electronic effects, steric effects, heteroatom tolerance, functional-group tolerance, product geometry, stereochemical transfer, and missing or incompatible classes.

## Comparison And Practicality

- When comparing methods, state the shared benchmark first and then identify the differentiating variable.
- Use like-for-like comparison axes: substrate class, product topology, activation mode, catalyst system, selectivity regime, mechanism evidence, functional-group tolerance, operational simplicity, sustainability, or downstream synthetic value.
- Use practicality adjectives only when the source provides operational evidence such as scale, low catalyst loading, simple workup, benign conditions, air/moisture tolerance, reaction time, solvent choice, or demonstrated derivatization.
- If a method requires high temperature, excess activator, specialized solvent, high loading, sensitive handling, or lacks scale data, keep that caveat near the claimed advantage.
- For improved-condition papers, keep the new and old reagent/catalyst combinations visible, such as `CuI/Cy2NH` versus `CuBr/i-Pr2NH`, when that contrast is the chemical point.
- For practical-protocol papers, keep the complete standard condition visible when it is the main contribution: catalyst loading, reagent equivalents, solvent, temperature, scale, and whether inert atmosphere is needed.
- Phrase advantages as class-specific when the data support only a class-specific benefit, for example terminal products with long carbon chains, rather than saying the new method is generally superior.
- When a substrate class is compatible but lower-yielding than a modified or previous protocol, write both halves: compatibility first, then the lower-yielding caveat.

## Organic Logic Transitions

Use these reusable transitions to turn source-paper facts into review prose. Replace the bracketed chemistry with the actual source-supported classes, catalysts, metrics, and limitations.

- **From inaccessible class to enabling design**
  - Use for protecting-group, directing-group, ligand, additive, solvent, or catalyst changes that open a previously missing substrate or product class.
  - Template: `To reach [previously inaccessible product/substrate class], [design element] was introduced into [reaction class], enabling [product class] from [starting materials] in [yield/selectivity].`
  - Example: `To reach primary alpha-allenols, a removable steric-control group can be described as the design element rather than as routine protection.`
- **From one substrate family to another**
  - Use when the important move is alkyl to aryl, terminal to internal, aldehyde to ketone, protected to unprotected, or simple to functionalized substrates.
  - Template: `The later method shifted the scope from [old class] to [new class], changing the relevant product description from [old product family] to [new product family].`
  - Example: `A terminal-alkyne method that gives 1,3-disubstituted allenes should not be summarized with allenol terminology from the preceding paragraph.`
- **From high selectivity to modest conversion or yield**
  - Use when ee, dr, rr, or de is strong but yield, conversion, or reproducibility is weak.
  - Template: `[Product class] was obtained with [high selectivity metric], although [yield/conversion/reproducibility limitation] restricts the practical claim.`
  - Example: `Excellent ee supports a stereochemical claim, but modest isolated yield blocks the adjective "efficient" unless the source gives a practical justification.`
- **From scope table to chemical trend**
  - Use when many entries differ only by substituent or chain length.
  - Template: `[Electronic/steric/functional-group class] substrates were tolerated, whereas [failed or weak class] defined the scope boundary.`
  - Example: `Halogenated and electronically varied aryl aldehydes may be grouped together, while a single aliphatic example should not become broad aliphatic scope.`
- **From condition optimization to mechanistic meaning**
  - Use when changed salt, ligand, additive, temperature, time, or sequence explains performance but does not prove a mechanism.
  - Template: `The dependence on [condition variable] indicates [reactivity/selectivity sensitivity] and was interpreted as [proposed role], rather than proving [mechanism].`
  - Example: `A protecting group's steric effect can be described as proposed or consistent with controls unless direct mechanistic evidence is supplied.`
- **From stereochemical inputs to stereodivergent outcome**
  - Use when product stereochemistry is controlled by catalyst enantiomer, chiral auxiliary, substrate chirality, matched/mismatched pairs, or combinations of central and axial chirality.
  - Template: `Changing [stereochemical input A] and [stereochemical input B] allowed access to [stereoisomer set], with [ee/de/dr] reported for the relevant comparison.`
  - Example: `If both central and axial chirality are discussed, specify which metric measures each relationship instead of saying "high stereoselectivity" alone.`
- **From synthetic utility to downstream value**
  - Use when the paper demonstrates derivatization or product utility.
  - Template: `[Product class] served as a precursor to [downstream class], showing utility beyond the formation step, but this does not expand the original reaction scope.`
  - Example: `Derivatization to amines or malonates demonstrates synthetic value; it does not imply those classes were direct reaction products.`
- **From contested literature result to corrected method**
  - Use when a paper reports that a previous method could not be reproduced, gives conflicting optical rotation or characterization data, or revises the operating system.
  - Template: `[Earlier authors] reported [method] for [product class], but [later source] could not reproduce [yield/selectivity] or found [characterization discrepancy]. A revised [catalyst/promoter/operation] then provided [product class] in [outcome], with the caveat or improvement stated on the same axis.`
  - Example: `If optical rotations disagree for products assigned similar ee, state this as a characterization discrepancy reported by the later source; do not imply misconduct or invalidate unrelated scope without evidence.`
- **From monocomponent to cooperative catalyst system**
  - Use when the advance is not a new substrate class alone, but division of labor between metals, catalysts, salts, ligands, or additives.
  - Template: `The [component A/component B] system improved [reactivity or selectivity] relative to [single component], and controls assigned [step A] mainly to [component A] while [component B or the combination] promoted [step B].`
  - Example: `A bimetallic allene synthesis should identify which component forms the propargylic amine and which component promotes amine-to-allene conversion when control experiments support that split.`
- **From general platform to subclass-tuned controller**
  - Use when a catalytic platform remains the same but a ligand, chiral amine, protecting group, additive, or temperature is changed for one substrate subclass.
  - Template: `For [substrate subclass], [new controller] replaced [standard controller] within the same [catalytic platform], improving [ee/yield/selectivity] for [product subclass].`
  - Example: `If chain length between an allene and a hydroxyl group changes enantioselectivity, describe the chiral-amine switch as subclass tuning rather than a new platform.`
- **From racemic baseline to later asymmetric platform**
  - Use at the beginning of a section when a racemic or non-asymmetric method establishes the parent transformation, reagent logic, and scope before later enantioselective variants are discussed.
  - Template: `The racemic version of [reaction class] was established by [key reagent/catalyst change], converting [starting class] to [product class] in [outcome] relative to [older protocol].`
  - Example: `A terminal-alkyne allenation section may begin with the Cu/amine/paraformaldehyde racemic method before discussing chiral amine variants.`
- **From parent substrate to heteroatom-derived substrate**
  - Use when an established reaction is applied to amides, alcohols, carbamates, malonates, carbohydrates, or other substrate classes that install a functional handle in the allene product.
  - Template: `[Parent reaction platform] was extended from [parent substrate class] to [heteroatom-derived substrate class], giving [functionalized product class] in [yield/scope].`
  - Example: `Applying a terminal-alkyne ATA protocol to N-propargyl amides should be written as synthesis of N-allenyl amides, not as another generic terminal-allene example.`
- **From intermediate synthesis to heterocycle applications**
  - Use when a prepared allene, allenol, allenamide, or propargylic derivative is then used to make heterocycles or other downstream products.
  - Template: `[Intermediate class] showed synthetic value through [cyclization/coupling/oxidation/substitution] to [downstream product class], while [intermediate synthesis] and [downstream transformation] remain separate claims.`
  - Example: `N-allenyl amides may be summarized as precursors to oxazolines or oxazines, but the heterocycle papers should support the application claim rather than the allene-preparation claim.`

## Selectivity And Stereochemistry

- Distinguish axial chirality from central chirality.
- Distinguish chirality transfer from catalyst-controlled enantioinduction.
- Use ee or er for enantiomeric control, dr for diastereomeric outcomes, and rr or regioisomeric ratio for positional selectivity.
- Use E/Z only for alkene geometry, not for allene axial chirality.
- If retention, erosion, racemization, or inversion of stereochemical information is relevant, state it explicitly.

## Allene And Propargylic Rules


### ATA Introduction And Crabbé Baseline Safeguards

- In an ATA review introduction, separate four citation roles: broad allene reactivity, axial-to-central chirality transfer, general allene synthesis, and catalytic asymmetric allene synthesis. These are background functions normally supported by review clusters; they do not establish the conditions or scope of the original ATA reaction.
- Do not state or imply that every allene is axially chiral. The two cumulene pi systems are orthogonal, but configurational axial chirality requires appropriately differentiated substituents at both terminal allene carbons; use `axially chiral allenes` or `appropriately substituted allenes` when making chirality-transfer claims.
- The occurrence of an allene motif in natural products or active pharmaceutical ingredients supports synthetic relevance, not biological activity, pharmacophore status, or a causal benefit of the allene unit unless those claims are independently demonstrated.
- Describe the original Crabbé-type transformation as homologation of a terminal 1-alkyne with paraformaldehyde in the presence of diisopropylamine and CuBr to give a monosubstituted terminal allene. Keep paraformaldehyde as the carbonyl/carbon-source partner and the secondary amine as a reaction component rather than calling it only a base.
- When reproducing the historical Scheme 1 conditions from the cited Account, retain CuBr at 0.33 equiv, diisopropylamine at 1.2 equiv, paraformaldehyde at 1.6 equiv, and refluxing 1,4-dioxane if exact method identity or later comparison requires them. Because the review labels the process `CuBr-mediated`, do not automatically rewrite it as a low-loading catalytic protocol.
- Use the 1979 papers for historical origin and the general acetylene-to-allene homologation. Use the 1984 fuller study for optimized substrate effects, extension to an aromatic acetylene and a diyne, labeling/mechanistic observations, and detailed limitations. Do not make the later study the discovery paper, and do not assign its entire scope analysis to the first steroid application report.
- State the substrate trend conservatively: alpha-hydroxy substitution increased reactivity in the original examples, whereas phenyl substitution and longer-chain alkyl substitution gave lower yields under those conditions. Do not convert this comparison into a universal directing-group, coordination, electronic, or chain-length rule without separate evidence.
- Preserve the nona-1,8-diyne outcome as two distinct products when it is used to expose the historical boundary: 6% monoallene and 16% bisallene in the cited review scheme. Do not write merely that the diyne was unreactive or gave a low overall yield.
- Keep product topology exact. Paraformaldehyde-based ATA gives monosubstituted terminal allenes; later normal-aldehyde ATA gives 1,3-disubstituted allenes, and ketone ATA gives trisubstituted allenes. Do not let the historical introduction blur these classes.
- The statement that ATA remained limited to paraformaldehyde for roughly three decades is a field-history conclusion supported by the allene-synthesis review literature cited as reference 3, not by the 1979 discovery papers alone.
- Do not import the later propargylic-amine/1,5-hydride-transfer mechanism into the historical introduction unless the paragraph is explicitly discussing the 1984 mechanistic study. The introduction's main function is to establish value, lineage, substrate sensitivity, and the paraformaldehyde bottleneck.

- Treat allenes as cumulenes with two orthogonal pi systems; use `allene`, `1,2-diene`, `terminal allene`, `internal allene`, `trisubstituted allene`, and `tetrasubstituted allene` only when the structure supports the term.
- Keep `propargyl`, `propargylic`, and `allenyl` distinct. `Propargylic` means adjacent to an alkyne; `propargyl` is a substituent label; `allenyl` means attached to or containing an allene fragment.
- Preserve derivative identity when it controls reactivity: propargylic alcohol, amine, carbonate, acetate, phosphate, halide, ether, protected alkynol, or unprotected alkynol.
- In ATA racemic-version summaries, define ATA by the transformation from terminal 1-alkynes to terminal or monosubstituted allenes before introducing later asymmetric variants.
- For paraformaldehyde-based ATA, preserve the complete reagent logic when it is the method identity: terminal 1-alkyne plus paraformaldehyde, secondary amine, and copper(I) salt in the specified solvent.
- When a source compares a modified ATA protocol with Crabbe-type conditions, state the concrete changed variables, such as amine and copper salt, before claiming improvement.
- When a later ATA paper converts a substoichiometric or stoichiometric metal/amine protocol into a catalytic, larger-scale, or operationally simpler procedure, summarize it as practicalization of the same terminal-alkyne-to-monosubstituted-allene platform rather than as a new reaction class.
- For catalytic practicalization of paraformaldehyde-based ATA, keep the standard condition visible when the source makes it the contribution: copper loading, paraformaldehyde and amine equivalents, solvent, temperature, reaction scale, and whether inert atmosphere is required.
- Do not write that a method is generally high-yielding if the source only says yields are higher than the original protocol. Pair the comparison with the actual yield range or representative threshold when available.
- For monosubstituted allene summaries, distinguish terminal allenes produced from terminal 1-alkynes and paraformaldehyde from 1,3-disubstituted allenes produced from aldehydes.
- When moving from paraformaldehyde-based ATA to ATA reactions with ordinary aldehydes, make the subsection transition explicit: paraformaldehyde gives terminal or monosubstituted allenes, whereas normal aldehydes introduce a second substituent and target 1,3-disubstituted allenes.
- For Au(I)- or Ag(I)-mediated precedents from Wong and Che, describe the demonstrated reaction as propargylic-amine-to-allene transformation or stereospecific rearrangement of optically active propargylic amines. Do not describe those papers as direct one-pot ATA reactions of terminal alkynes, aldehydes, and amines unless the source actually performs that sequence.
- In normal-aldehyde ATA mechanism summaries, keep the two stages separate: first, terminal alkyne, aldehyde, and secondary amine generate a propargylic amine through an alkynyl-metal/iminium pathway; second, metal coordination to the propargylic amine alkyne enables proposed 1,5-hydride transfer and beta-N elimination to give the allene.
- When prior Au/Ag propargylic amine papers are cited as mechanistic precedents, use them to support alkyne coordination, intramolecular hydride transfer, and beta-elimination only at the propargylic-amine-to-allene stage. Treat the upstream iminium/alkynyl-metal formation as the new ATA design unless the cited source directly studies it.
- If a review paragraph calls the normal-aldehyde ATA process redox, preserve the species-level statement: the secondary amine is oxidized to an imine byproduct during allene formation. Do not transfer that redox claim to every Au/Ag propargylic amine paper unless the source states the same oxidation event.
- In section-opening paragraphs, compress ee, axial chirality, microwave acceleration, and artemisinin-derivative examples from the Au/Ag precedent papers unless those details explain the new normal-aldehyde ATA design. Their main review role is precedent for 1,3-disubstituted allene formation from propargylic amines.
- For the first ZnI2/morpholine normal-aldehyde ATA examples, describe the reaction as ZnI2-mediated or ZnI2-promoted when high ZnI2 loading is required; do not call it catalytic merely because the proposed mechanism regenerates ZnI2.
- In ZnI2/morpholine ATA scope summaries, separate the productive parent scope from weaker boundary classes: terminal alkyl alkynes with benzaldehyde or electron-poor aryl aldehydes give 1-aryl-3-alkylallenes in moderate yields, whereas heteroaryl aldehydes and aliphatic aldehydes should be kept as lower-yielding or more demanding examples.
- When ZnI2/morpholine ATA is applied to terminal propargylic alcohols or terminal propargylic sulfonamides/sulfamides, describe the products as 1,3-disubstituted alpha-allenols or 2,3-allenyl sulfonamide/sulfamide derivatives. Keep these as synthetically useful functionalized subclasses rather than merging them with simple hydrocarbon allene scope.
- If propargylic alcohol products are obtained as diastereomer mixtures, mention this only when selectivity is review-relevant; otherwise the paragraph may compress them as functionalized 1,3-disubstituted allenes in moderate yields.
- For ZnI2/morpholine normal-aldehyde ATA mechanism summaries, keep the mechanism proposed: alkynyl zinc formation, iminium addition to a propargylic amine, ZnI2 coordination to the alkyne, 1,5-hydride transfer, and beta-elimination are a mechanistic framework unless stronger evidence is supplied.
- Use propargylic amine intermediate experiments, water-addition effects, and no-ZnI2 controls as support for the proposed pathway and ZnI2 requirement, but do not treat them as complete proof of catalyst deactivation or turnover failure.
- When explaining why catalytic ZnI2 loading was not achieved, state the authors' rationale as a limitation, such as sequestration by in situ water or imine/byproduct coordination, and keep it distinct from experimentally demonstrated scope.
- For CuI-catalyzed normal-aldehyde ATA developed after ZnI2-promoted or microwave precedents, frame the method as a catalytic/practical repair only when the source demonstrates low copper loading, such as 10 mol% CuI. Keep earlier microwave or high-loading reports as comparison baselines rather than sources for the new scope.
- In CuI/dialkylamine ATA summaries, treat the amine as a structure-sensitive reaction component, not a generic base. Preserve the winning amine for the representative aldehyde when it defines the method, such as dibutylamine for n-butanal-derived allenols.
- When a dialkylamine and aldehyde are matched, state the match/mismatch logic if it affects product identity: hydrolysis of the in situ generated imine can regenerate an aldehyde corresponding to the amine alkyl group, leading to a mixture of allenes if that aldehyde differs from the intended aldehyde.
- If a common amine such as diisobutylamine is introduced to avoid contaminating allenes from the amine-derived aldehyde, write it as an operational compromise: broader convenience or cleaner product identity with slightly lower yields.
- For CuI-catalyzed functionalized allene synthesis from terminal alkynes and aliphatic aldehydes, distinguish the direct product classes as alkyl-substituted 1,3-disubstituted allenes, alpha-allenols, allenyl amides, allenyl sulfonamides, or ether-containing allenes according to the substrate table.
- When comparing the CuI catalytic protocol with the ZnI2-promoted normal-aldehyde ATA, bind the advantage to the demonstrated axis, especially better preparation of alkyl-substituted 1,3-disubstituted allenes or aliphatic-aldehyde-derived functionalized allenes. Do not state a universal superiority over the ZnI2 method.
- If terminal aryl alkynes are described as incompatible or still outside the scope, keep this as a substrate boundary. When the primary source only shows absence from scope or indirect comparison, avoid adding a mechanistic explanation for the failure.
- Do not merge THIQ-mediated mild-condition allene synthesis with CuI/dialkylamine catalytic ATA. THIQ protocols use a telescoped sequence with CuBr-mediated propargylamine formation followed by ZnI2-mediated allene formation under milder conditions; cite or summarize them as a later operational variant, not as evidence for the CuI/dibutylamine scope.
- For ATA reactions with ketones, frame the advance as access to trisubstituted allenes from a less reactive carbonyl partner. Do not describe the method as another normal-aldehyde ATA example or as a route to 1,3-disubstituted allenes.
- When ketone ATA is enabled by CdI2/pyrrolidine, describe CdI2 as a mediator or promoter if used in high loading, such as 0.8 equiv. Do not call the system catalytic unless the source demonstrates catalytic turnover.
- In ketone ATA section openings, preserve the metal-family screening if it explains the breakthrough: group 11/12 salts such as CuI, ZnI2, AgI, AuI, and HgCl2 were ineffective or gave mainly intermediate/trace allene, whereas CdI2 gave the trisubstituted allene under the matched amine conditions.
- Treat pyrrolidine as the matched amine for the CdI2-mediated ketone ATA only when the source uses it in the optimized system. Do not generalize pyrrolidine to aldehyde ATA or CuI/dialkylamine variants.
- For CdI2-mediated ketone ATA scope summaries, distinguish terminal aryl alkynes, heteroaryl alkynes, and terminal alkyl alkynes, and distinguish cyclic ketones from acyclic unsymmetrical ketones when those axes define yield or stereochemical outcomes.
- Compress the ketone ATA product scope by product topology: trisubstituted allenes as the parent class, 1,5-bisallenes as a bis-allene extension, and protected alpha- or beta-allenols as functionalized allene products.
- For protected chiral allenol examples in ketone ATA, preserve the protecting group only when it defines the product or chirality-transfer example, such as TBS-protected or acetyl-protected allenols. Report ee or dr only if the paragraph discusses stereochemical retention or induction.
- If a nonsymmetrical ketone gives a 1:1 diastereomer mixture or no 1,2-chiral induction, keep that as a stereochemical boundary rather than describing the method as broadly stereoselective.
- In CdI2-mediated ketone ATA mechanism summaries, keep the propargylic amine intermediate evidence separate from the full mechanism: low-temperature intermediate observation and conversion of isolated propargylic amines support the proposed pathway, while alkynyl cadmium, ketoniminium addition, CdI2 coordination, 1,5-hydride transfer, and beta-elimination remain a proposed mechanism unless directly established.
- When comparing metal roles in ketone ATA, state the role split precisely if supported: CuI may form propargylic amine but not the allene product, whereas CdI2 promotes both propargylic amine formation and the propargylic amine-to-allene conversion under the reported conditions.
- For ketone-based routes that combine a KA2 reaction with an amine-to-allene conversion, keep the two transformations distinct. KA2 forms tertiary propargylic amines from terminal alkynes, ketones, and amines; the subsequent ATA-type step converts those propargylic amines into trisubstituted allenes.
- If CuBr-catalyzed KA2 and ZnI2-promoted propargylic-amine-to-allene conversion are joined because both work in toluene, describe the result as a filtered/telescoped two- or three-step route when Cu(I) must be removed before the ZnI2 step. Do not call this a direct catalytic one-pot ATA protocol.
- In CuBr KA2 summaries used as a precursor to ketone ATA, cite the KA2 source only for propargylic amine formation: CuBr in toluene enables ketone-alkyne-amine coupling and can cover methyl ketones, ethyl/dialkyl ketones, and cyclic ketones according to the source table. Do not use it as evidence for allene formation.
- In ZnI2-promoted trisubstituted-allene summaries from propargylic amines, preserve the ZnI2 role as the downstream amine-to-allene conversion. If the source reports that direct combination with the CuBr KA2 step gave poor results and filtration through silica gel was essential to remove Cu(I), keep that operation as the reason the route remains telescoped.
- When an earlier ketone ATA platform is described as narrow, name the productive ketone classes that define the boundary, such as cyclic ketones and methyl alkyl ketones, before introducing a later method that extends to aryl alkyl ketones or dialkyl ketones.
- For direct catalytic ketone ATA with CuI/ZnBr2/Ti(OEt)4, preserve the complete cooperative system when it is the advance: CuI loading, ZnBr2 amount, Ti(OEt)4 amount, solvent, and temperature if the paragraph is discussing practicality or comparison. Do not reduce the method to only CuI catalysis or only ZnBr2 promotion.
- In CuI/ZnBr2/Ti(OEt)4 ketone ATA control-experiment summaries, assign roles by step: Ti(OEt)4 is required for propargylic amine formation but does not participate in the isolated propargylic-amine-to-allene conversion; CuI is more efficient than ZnBr2 for propargylic amine formation; ZnBr2 is more effective than CuI for converting the propargylic amine to the allene.
- Treat the CuI/ZnBr2/Ti(OEt)4 protocol as a direct one-pot repair of earlier CdI2-mediated or CuBr/ZnI2 filtered routes only on the demonstrated axes: broader ketone scope and removal of the intermediate filtration requirement. Keep any remaining limitations or moderate yields if the source reports them.
- Functional-group tolerance in ATA should name the tolerated substrate functionalities, such as mesylate, hydroxyl, ether, or amide, and should not imply those functional groups are newly installed in the reaction.
- Large-scale examples in racemic ATA papers support practicality of representative substrates; do not let them expand the functional-group scope beyond the substrate table.
- For ATA extension to N-propargyl amides, describe the products as terminal N-allenyl amides or N-(buta-2,3-dienyl) amides, and keep the amide substituent scope separate from ordinary hydrocarbon terminal-allene scope.
- When a Cy2NH/CuI ATA protocol is compared with Hashmi's use of Crabbe-type i-Pr2NH/CuBr conditions, attach the comparison only to N-allenyl amide preparation and state that the advantage is higher yield on that substrate class.
- Do not use a gold-catalyzed oxazine paper as evidence for the yield of a later CuI/Cy2NH allene-amide synthesis, except as the external benchmark if the source explicitly reports the earlier allene-amide yields.
- Distinguish N-allenyl amides as synthetic intermediates from their downstream heterocycles. Oxazolines from Pd-catalyzed coupling-cyclization and 1,3-oxazines from gold-catalyzed cyclization are application products, not the direct products of ATA.
- For paragraphs that cite several oxazoline papers, group them as later synthetic applications only if they share the same intermediate class. Keep differences in activation mode, such as Pd coupling-cyclization versus electrophilic cyclization, out of a single unsupported mechanism claim.
- If a paragraph says N-allenyl amides show synthetic potential, name the demonstrated product family, such as oxazolines or oxazines, rather than using broad value language alone.
- For allenol summaries, preserve position and chain-placement categories when they define scope, such as alpha-allenols, beta-allenols, and allenols bearing a long carbon chain between the allene unit and the hydroxyl group.
- For terminal-allene protocol summaries, distinguish functionalized terminal allenes, protected or unprotected alpha-allenols, alpha-allenylamides, and nonfunctionalized 1-alkyne-derived allenes when those classes define the scope.
- In catalytic terminal-allene protocol summaries, put functionalized monosubstituted allene classes before simple nonfunctionalized 1-alkynes when the source's value claim is functional-group compatibility. Treat nonfunctionalized 1-alkynes as a compatibility boundary if the source reports them as lower-yielding than an earlier modified protocol.
- For protected and unprotected alpha-allenol examples, keep the protection state as a scope axis, not as a mechanism or directing effect, unless the source provides separate mechanistic evidence.
- For alpha-allenylamide preparation under ATA conditions, describe the products as alpha-allenylamides or terminal allene amide derivatives according to the source structure, and keep their performance separate from nonfunctionalized terminal allene examples.
- For buta-2,3-dien-1-ol or other small allenol building-block summaries, separate three evidence roles: application papers show synthetic or biological utility, older synthesis papers show access limitations, and the preparation paper or checked procedure supports the new conditions and yields.
- When introducing buta-2,3-dien-1-ol, identify it as the simplest alpha-allenol only if the source or review context uses that relationship; do not infer that all alpha-allenol scope applies to this parent reagent.
- If old buta-2,3-dien-1-ol routes use but-2-yne-1,4-diol, chlorinated intermediates, LiAlH4, MeLi, or other hazardous or inconvenient operations, state the drawback only at the level supported by the source, such as multistep preparation, harmful large-scale operation, explosive intermediate, or dangerous reductant.
- For practical buta-2,3-dien-1-ol procedures derived from ATA chemistry, preserve THF versus dioxane when solvent choice solves workup or separation, and keep reflux/open-flask and high-pressure autoclave procedures as distinct options rather than merging their catalyst loadings and yield ranges.

### Chirality-Transfer And Organometallic Allene Supplements

Use these rules for review paragraphs covering optically active allenes, propargylic substitutions, organocopper or organozinc allene synthesis, alpha-allenols, and catalytic asymmetric propargylic substitution.

- Preserve optical rotation as the measured property reported by the source. Do not translate it into ee, er, enantiopurity, optical purity, or a qualitative enantioselectivity category without an independent composition measurement or validated correlation.
- A single asymmetric preparation demonstrating enantiomorphic allene forms proves stereochemical possibility, not broad substrate scope or generally effective synthesis.
- Separate these evidence layers: existence of allene axial chirality, preparation of optically active material, measurement of enantiomeric enrichment, assignment of absolute configuration, and proof of reaction mechanism.
- In asymmetric synthesis reviews, distinguish invention of a new ligand skeleton, modification of an established scaffold, screening of known ligands, and first application of a known ligand to a reaction class.
- Treat development of a racemic allene-forming protocol and development of its enantioselective variant as separate advances unless the same source directly establishes both.
- A field-progress statement for asymmetric allene synthesis should identify product topology, stereochemical metric, and remaining substrate or accessibility boundary.
- Report central-to-axial or point-to-axial chirality-transfer efficiency only when stereochemical composition is available for both the propargylic precursor and the allene product under compatible definitions.
- Distinguish reactions using stoichiometric lithium diorganocuprates from copper-catalyzed substitutions using substoichiometric copper and a separate organometallic nucleophile.
- Attribute each propargylic leaving-group class to sources that actually examined it. Do not convert a later platform inventory into the scope of a founding acetate, carbonate, phosphate, halide, ether, mesylate, or sulfonate study.
- State whether allene absolute configuration was determined directly, correlated chemically, assigned by an empirical rule, or inferred from reaction stereochemistry.
- Do not use an `anti-SN2'` or related label to erase evidence for stepwise organometallic pathways. Overall stereochemical displacement is not direct proof of a concerted elementary mechanism.
- In copper-catalyzed propargylic ether and epoxide reactions, preserve the identity of RMgCl, RMgBr, RMgI, or another organometallic reagent when it changes elimination geometry, syn/anti outcome, or configuration transfer.
- Describe allenylcopper(III), propargylcopper, or other high-valent organocopper intermediates as proposed or indirectly supported unless directly observed. Trapping products, reduction products, and stereochemical correlations do not by themselves constitute direct observation.
- Do not assign propargylic epoxides exclusively to one pathway when sources support both copper(III)-type and addition-elimination pathways under different organometallic conditions.
- For organocopper-based allene synthesis, report product-exposure controls when using isolated-product ee, optical yield, or rotation to evaluate chirality transfer.
- Copper-catalyzed conversion of an enantioenriched propargylic phosphate is a point-to-axial chirality-transfer process, not catalytic asymmetric induction from an achiral precursor.
- Preserve whether the carbon partner is an alkyl-9-BBN reagent, arylboronate, alkenylboronate, arylboronic ester, Grignard reagent, organolithium reagent, organozinc reagent, or other nucleophile because catalysts, additives, temperature, solvent, and scope may differ.
- For propargylic-to-allene couplings, report gamma/alpha regioselectivity independently from product ee. High axial selectivity does not establish exclusive allene-versus-alkyne formation.
- Preserve scope failures involving secondary alkylboranes, terminal-alkyne phosphates, bulky propargylic substituents, alternative leaving groups, sensitive boronate esters, or other weakened partner classes when assessing generality.
- Under strongly basic organometallic conditions, distinguish a nominal free alcohol in the starting material from its deprotonated or metal-coordinated form during reaction.
- For alpha-allenol synthesis, report chemical yield and axial stereochemical outcome independently, including how each was measured.
- Justify alpha-allenol utility with source-supported transformations enabled by the combined allene and hydroxyl functionalities rather than broad value language alone.
- Do not use final allenol optical activity as a direct measure of the bond-forming step when the organometallic system may racemize the allene product.
- In stereospecific organozinc allene synthesis, preserve whether the reagent is LiCl-free or contains LiCl, because salt-containing preparations may racemize the allene even when substitution still occurs.
- Attribute DMSO- or donor-solvent-promoted SN2'-type scope specifically to the leaving group actually tested, such as propargylic mesylates, unless other leaving groups are directly examined.
- When allene configuration or stereochemical purity is established through conversion to a known chiral derivative, identify the correlation sequence and avoid presenting it as direct allene analysis.
- Preserve donor-solvent identity in organozinc reactions when it changes reagent nucleophilicity and determines whether substitution occurs. Do not call DMSO activation a pure polarity effect unless the data distinguish polarity from donor ability.
- Preserve the 1,1-dichloropropargylic motif when it defines substrate prochirality, leaving-group behavior, chloroallene formation, and downstream functionalization.
- In Cu-catalyzed asymmetric propargylic substitution, report copper source, ligand, solvent, and temperature as a matched system when changing solvent reverses or strongly changes the effect of ligand variants.
- Preserve sterically demanding nucleophiles that give racemic or moderate-ee product as stereochemical scope boundaries even when conversion or regioselectivity remains acceptable.
- Treat an enantioenriched chloroallene as a branch-point intermediate only when each downstream product topology, reagent class, solvent, and ee-retention result is separately documented.
- Distinguish conversion of an allene axis into a terminal alkyne bearing a central propargylic quaternary stereocenter from simple preservation of axial chirality.
- Do not claim a proved enantioinduction mechanism from ligand-screening data alone.

### Palladium, Carbonylation, Hydrozirconation, And Au/Ag Allene Supplements

Use these rules for Pd-catalyzed propargylic couplings and carbonylations, asymmetric protonation of allenylmetal species, Mitsunobu-fragmentation allene synthesis, hydrozirconation of propargylic alcohols, ATA platform comparisons, and Au/Ag-mediated propargylamine-to-allene conversion.

- In Pd-catalyzed propargylic coupling, preserve the exact carbonate, acetate, carboxylate, phosphate, mesylate, or other leaving group because it can change product ee from high to nearly racemic.
- Treat substitution at the alkyne terminus or internal-alkyne substitution as a separate stereochemical-risk class when it correlates with allenylpalladium isomerization and severe ee erosion.
- Record base identity, water ratio, and temperature when basic aqueous conditions suppress a proposed acid-assisted racemization pathway.
- Keep organozinc, organoindium, arylboronic-acid, and other Pd coupling partners separate because transmetalation rate, side reactions, and scope boundaries differ.
- An overall anti or syn stereochemical relationship does not establish complete point-to-axial chirality transfer.
- In propargylic carbonylation, preserve whether the alkyne is terminal or internally substituted because this can control the configurational lifetime of the allenylpalladium manifold.

- Pd-catalyzed conversion of racemic propargylic carbonates with a chiral ligand is catalytic asymmetric axial induction, not point-to-axial transfer from an enantioenriched substrate.
- Preserve which ligand enantiomer furnishes which allene axial configuration and the paired yield/ee comparison when opposite ligand enantiomers are tested.
- Describe demonstrated racemic-carbonate scope by the actual substrate class, such as internally substituted propargylic carbonates. Do not expand to terminal carbonates without primary evidence.
- Enrichment of recovered racemic substrate signals kinetic resolution and must qualify a simple dynamic kinetic asymmetric transformation description.
- Enantioselective protonation of racemic allenylmetal species should not be described as central-to-axial chirality transfer.
- Preserve the identity and equivalents of the chiral proton source because it is the stereochemistry-determining reagent when a metal is catalytic but the proton donor is stoichiometric.
- Separate Pd-mediated propargyl/allenyl activation, SmI2-mediated reduction or umpolung, and chiral-alcohol protonation in Pd/Sm-mediated allenoate formation.
- Record any alkynyl ester formed alongside an allenoate when evaluating regioselectivity.
- Identify lanthanide-induced-shift NMR, chiral-shift NMR, HPLC, GC, optical rotation, or chemical correlation as the stereochemical measurement method actually used.
- Describe propargylic alcohol-to-allene NBSH chemistry as sequential Mitsunobu formation and sulfonylhydrazine fragmentation. Retain phosphine, azodicarboxylate, NBSH, reagent order, and temperature stages.
- Preserve order of addition when premature NBSH degradation produces propargylic sulfinate esters instead of the desired allene.
- Separate broad chemical scope in propargylic alcohol-to-allene conversion from the much smaller set with quantitatively paired precursor and product ee.
- Loss of sulfinic acid and dinitrogen supports sulfonylhydrazine fragmentation, but exact hydrogen-transfer topology requires separate evidence.
- For hydrozirconation/elimination of propargylic alcohols, preserve the substrate-dependent division between magnesium alkoxide and zinc alkoxide protocols.
- Record zinc-salt stoichiometry when excess zinc causes post-formation allene racemization.
- Preserve over-reduction under magnesium conditions, polymerization of terminal allenes, and other side reactions as condition-selection boundaries.
- Distinguish rapid elimination from internal propargylic-alcohol-derived vinylzirconium species from the greater stability of typical terminal analogues, while preserving reported exceptions.
- Describe cis Zr-H addition and syn zirconoxy elimination as stereochemically consistent proposals unless direct intermediate evidence is available.
- Preserve the ATA carbonyl-to-product topology map: paraformaldehyde gives monosubstituted terminal allenes, ordinary aldehydes give 1,3-disubstituted allenes, and ketones give trisubstituted allenes.
- Treat the secondary amine in ATA as a reaction component involved in iminium/propargylic-amine formation and proposed hydride delivery, not as an interchangeable base.
- Use `mediated`, `promoted`, or high-loading terminology for ATA systems employing about one-third to four-fifths equivalent metal salt unless catalytic turnover is independently established.
- Preserve water loading when it substantially changes conversion of a propargylic amine to an allene in a metal-mediated ATA system.
- Retention of a pre-existing central stereocenter and a 1:1 dr for newly formed axial configurations must be reported as distinct stereochemical outcomes.
- Describe metal-coordinated `[1,5]-hydride transfer` and beta-elimination in ATA as proposed unless hydrogen-origin, crossover, isotope, kinetic, or pathway-specific evidence is available.
- In Au-salen A3 coupling followed by propargylamine-to-allene conversion, identify chiral prolinol or related amine as the stoichiometric stereochemical source rather than attributing asymmetric induction to the metal catalyst.
- Report non-diaryl gold products as chemically accessible while separately identifying the narrower class that undergoes high center-to-axis transfer.
- Preserve high Ag(I) loading, darkness, solvent, temperature, and thermal or microwave mode when defining silver propargylamine-to-allene procedures.
- For propargylamine-to-allene rearrangement, report conversion, isolated-yield basis, and ee as separate metrics.
- Deuterium retention plus absence of crossover strongly supports intramolecular hydrogen transfer but does not independently prove the complete beta-elimination sequence.
- Describe KAuCl4 or another charged Au(III) source as the precatalyst and Au(I) as a proposed reactive species when supported by ESI-MS, comparative catalyst experiments, or speciation evidence.

### EATA, Enyne Addition, Rh Addition, And Ketene-Allenoate Supplements

Use these rules for EATA variants, ligand-controlled allene relays, Pd-catalyzed enyne hydroaddition, Rh-catalyzed enynone or enynamide 1,6-addition, and ketene/HWE allenoate synthesis.

- Describe EATA using an equivalent or more of a chiral amino alcohol as stoichiometric chiral-reagent control, not catalytic asymmetric induction, when that reagent supplies the stereochemical information.
- For protected alpha-oxy terminal alkynes, separate steric bulk, oxygen functionality, protecting-group identity, and chain topology. Do not reduce success to one structural feature when several variables change together.
- When higher temperature improves EATA yield or conversion but lowers allene ee, report both conditions as a yield-ee-temperature tradeoff.
- A low-yield simple-terminal-alkyne experiment with no ee measurement supports a reactivity boundary but not an enantioselectivity boundary.
- Trace allene formation despite consumption of free propargylic alcohol indicates competing pathways; do not label the free alcohol simply unreactive.
- Keep staged zinc, cooperative-metal, and protecting-group-directed EATA repair papers as separate branches of one EATA program.
- Describe sequential addition and staged heating accurately; do not imply isolation of a propargylic intermediate when none occurred.
- Preserve original EATA performance and later failed reproduction as parallel experimental records. Do not treat the failed reproduction as proof that the original report was false unless the source establishes the cause.
- Keep specific rotation and chiral-chromatographic ee as distinct measurements; disagreement requires investigation rather than direct conversion.
- Assign CuBr/ZnBr2 roles only to the extent supported by single-metal and isolated-intermediate controls. One metal may favor propargylic-amine formation and the other amine-to-allene conversion, but do not make either role exclusive unless proven.
- When two solvent or temperature sets trade yield against ee in a bimetallic EATA system, report them as separate condition branches rather than one optimum.
- Tie aldehyde incompatibility to the exact EATA protocol; failure in one system does not establish a class-wide prohibition.
- State when primary alpha-allenol yields cover EATA plus deprotection rather than the protected allene-forming step alone.
- Describe TBS or another protecting group as a balance of protection, steric influence, stability, and removability, not as evidence that maximal bulk is best.
- For secondary alpha-allenols, preserve central configuration, axial configuration, crude dr, axis-related de, and ee separately.
- Unchanged ee on prolonged heating supports stability of the tested protected-product system but should not be generalized to all allenols.
- When a protecting-group-enabled EATA protocol requires substantial mediator loading, stoichiometric chiral controller, high temperature, and deprotection, preserve those burdens.
- Restrict demonstrated EATA aldehyde scope to the classes shown in the source and mark failed classes `missing_source` when documented only in unavailable SI.
- Describe a Cu/chiral-ligand A3 coupling followed by Cu removal and zinc-mediated allene formation as a filtered sequential process, not a direct one-pot ATA.
- Preserve proposed stage-specific hydroxyl coordination to different metals as inference unless directly observed.
- Record which ligand stereoisomer gives which allene-axis configuration; do not reduce the source to an unspecified chiral ligand.
- Mark primary-propargylic-alcohol incompatibility `missing_source` for a ligand-controlled branch unless direct evidence is available.
- Trace-product controls with gold or silver establish failure for the tested hydroxyl-functionalized propargylic amine, not propargylic amines generally.
- Access to all four center/axis stereoisomers requires both substrate enantiomers and ligand or chiral-reagent enantiomers; ligand switching alone is insufficient.
- In enyne hydroaddition, preserve the requirement for a sufficiently hindered alkyne-terminal substituent when less hindered enynes give poor allene selectivity.
- Record failure of alkene-substituted enyne frameworks when the source shows that a parent buten-yne topology is required.
- In Pd-catalyzed enyne hydroboration or hydrosilylation, report allenyl-product selectivity separately from total hydrometallation yield, including dienyl, overaddition, or other competing products.
- Use `estimated` or `at least` language when allenylborane ee is inferred from a downstream alcohol rather than measured directly.
- Treat a high-ee ligand result on one model enyne as a benchmark rather than broad-scope evidence.
- When cooling improves enyne hydrosilylation ee but lowers yield and lengthens reaction time, report all three metrics.
- For Rh-catalyzed enynone 1,6-addition, preserve enynone topology, organotitanium speciation, Rh precursor, chiral ligand, trapping reagent, loading, solvent, temperature, and time.
- Distinguish initially generated allenyl silyl enol ether from the stable derivative used for isolation and chiral analysis.
- Apply high-ee claims to the complete substituent/core framework; do not attribute selectivity to one terminal substituent when ring modification lowers ee.
- Preserve the distinction between ionic aryltitanate and neutral aryltitanium reagents when they produce different addition topologies.
- Retain catalyst-free allene formation when interpreting Rh catalyst loading and observed ee.
- Record conversion of an allenyl ketone into a conjugated diene during workup or chromatography as a product-stability limitation.
- Report alkenylrhodium and oxa-pi-allylrhodium species as proposed intermediates and the isomerization as a proposed enantiodetermining step unless directly observed.
- In Rh-catalyzed enynamide 1,6-addition, preserve substrate-analogue controls showing that an amide or related directing group suppresses competing addition topology.
- Describe tested trialkylsilyl groups and failed nonsilyl comparisons; do not convert this into a maximal-bulk rule.
- When ferrocenyl or other ligand substituents mainly affect conversion, distinguish that activity effect from their less-isolated contribution to ee.
- Restrict nucleophile scope to demonstrated arylboronic acids when an alkenylboronic acid fails; do not generalize to all organoboron reagents.

- Do not present one phosphine oxide recovery, reduction, and reuse experiment as repeated phosphine or catalyst recycling.
- When propargyl and allenyl anions are drawn as a common framework, state whether they are resonance representations, equilibrating intermediates, or separately supported species; do not infer this status from a review scheme.
- When electrophilic trapping may furnish propargylic and allenic products, identify central and axial chirality separately and retain the regioselectivity evidence that determines product topology.
- A racemic phase-transfer-catalyzed alkyne isomerization table does not establish asymmetric scope when only one substrate is tested with a chiral catalyst.
- In chiral PTC isomerization, retain the distinction between catalytic quaternary ammonium salt and stoichiometric inorganic base when describing catalyst loading, mildness, or operational economy.

- When an ester, protecting group, or substituent is required for high stereocontrol, describe it as a substrate-design requirement rather than routine decoration.
- In allenoate isomerization scope summaries, retain explicitly slow, unsuccessful, low-conversion, or low-isolation substrate classes even when other entries show high ee.
- In tandem activated-enyne addition/allenoate synthesis, distinguish the catalyst's role in C-C bond formation from its role in stereocontrolled alkynoate-to-allenoate proton transfer.
- When an allenoate is isolated with an alkynoate intermediate, preserve the allenoate/alkynoate ratio and specify whether it refers to crude material, an isolated mixture, or material after secondary isomerization.
- Scope summaries for nitroalkane addition to activated enynes must retain the alpha-nitroalkyl substituent introduced by the nucleophile; do not generalize the family to all trisubstituted 2,3-allenoates.

- For ambident allene-derived anions, identify whether electrophilic attack gives an axially chiral allene or a centrally chiral alkyne, and retain the allene/alkyne ratio.
- In tetrasubstituted-allene synthesis, keep regioselectivity, diastereoselectivity, and enantioselectivity orthogonal and identify the product, regioisomer, or diastereomer to which each value applies.

### Resolution, Pd Allenyl Substitution, Bromodiene, And Beta-Elimination Supplements

- For enzymatic or chemical kinetic resolution, evaluate selectivity factor, conversion, and ee together. Specify whether each ee belongs to recovered starting material or transformed product.
- In dynamic kinetic resolution, distinguish the stereoselective resolution step from the in situ racemization step and identify which catalyst performs each operation.
- In DKR summaries, evaluate yield, enantiopurity, differential substrate/product racemization, and catalyst compatibility rather than ee alone.
- For lipase-catalyzed desymmetrization of prochiral allenic diols, preserve monoacylation, the remaining free alcohol, allene substitution topology, and competing diacylation.
- Do not call allenic-diol desymmetrization kinetic resolution unless the source actually resolves a racemate.
- For classical resolution of axially chiral allenoic acids by chiral-amine salts, keep salt formation, recrystallization, free-acid release, and downstream chirality-transfer reactions as separate stages.
- In allenoic-acid resolution, identify whether ee belongs to the axially chiral acid or to a centrally chiral downstream derivative.
- For kinetic resolution of 2,3-allenoates by cycloaddition, preserve recovered allenoate and cycloadduct as distinct product branches and do not merge their yield or ee ranges.
- Distinguish benzyl and directly aryl-substituted allenoates; do not convert an untested direct aryl class into a failed class.
- For clustered Pd-catalyzed substitution of allenyl electrophiles, preserve a matrix of electrophile class, nucleophile class, ligand system, allene substituent, product topology, yield, ee, and dr.
- Do not combine allenyl phosphates and acetates, malonates and amines, or monoallene and bis-allene products into one undifferentiated Pd substitution scope.
- When an allene reaction controls both axial and central stereochemistry, report axial ee separately from dr or de between central/axial stereoisomers.
- Matched/mismatched chiral-substrate de or dr does not describe a racemic-substrate scope.
- For Pd-catalyzed asymmetric allene formation from 2-bromo-1,3-dienes, keep electrophile substitution, nucleophile class, ligand, base, decisive additive, product subclass, isolated yield, and ee as one linked record.
- Do not discuss a bromodiene-platform ee maximum without the corresponding catalytic activity, yield, and competing-product profile.
- For allene synthesis proposed to occur by beta-hydride elimination from vinylpalladium, preserve substrate alkene geometry, accessible beta-hydrogen arrangement, catalyst coordination environment, temperature, allene substitution, and hydropalladation or racemization evidence.
- Distinguish product isolation from direct observation of a vinylpalladium intermediate or beta-elimination event.
- Generalize ligand effects only with the associated metal, electrophile, nucleophile, product topology, conditions, and performance metric.
- Future-direction claims in allene methodology must define starting-material class, bond-forming event, substitution level, stereochemical target, and competing pathway to suppress.
- For proposed beta-elimination routes to allenes, distinguish direct allene product evidence from direct evidence for the organometallic intermediate, elimination step, and catalyst-regeneration process.

## Prohibited Patterns

Avoid these unless the paper directly supports the exact claim:

- "has attracted increasing attention"
- "recent years have seen"
- "interesting reactivity"
- "mild conditions" without condition context
- "good yield", "excellent selectivity", "high efficiency", or "broad scope" without numbers or demonstrated scope classes
- "proceeds smoothly"
- "novel" without naming the novelty
- "to the best of our knowledge"
- "review articles demonstrate" for primary experimental outcomes
- "propargyl alcohol" when the intended class is substituted propargylic alcohols or derivatives
- "allene E/Z selectivity"

## One-Paragraph Organic Review Pattern

Prefer this information order:

1. Transformation identity and source contribution.
2. Catalyst/activation/substrate/product classes.
3. Quantitative outcome and scope boundary.
4. Mechanistic evidence or author-proposed rationale.
5. Limitation or reason the paper matters to the review section.

The paragraph should read as synthesis of evidence, not as a paper abstract or a list of experimental details.

Use a compact rhythm:

```text
[Method identity and product topology]. [Key evidence plus scope or selectivity compression]. [Mechanistic or comparison qualifier]. [Limitation-linked takeaway].
```
